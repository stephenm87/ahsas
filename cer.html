Claims, Evidence & Reasoning

The Foundation of Historical Argumentation

CER is a framework for building strong historical arguments. Every analytical paragraph starts with a Claim, supports it with Evidence, and explains the connection through Reasoning.

📝 Claims What you argue

A claim is an arguable statement that directly answers a historical question. It is not a fact — it's your interpretation that must be defended with evidence and reasoning.

What makes a strong claim?

  • Directly answers the question — no restating or summarizing
  • Takes a clear position that someone could disagree with
  • Specific enough to be proven with evidence, broad enough to develop
  • Uses PIECES themes for analytical precision
Example

✗ Weak "The Mongol Empire was important for trade."

✓ Strong "The Mongol Empire's enforcement of the Pax Mongolica fundamentally restructured Eurasian economic systems (ECN) by securing overland trade routes, which in turn accelerated cultural exchange (CDI) between East Asia and the Islamic world."

📚 Evidence What you use to prove it

Evidence is the specific historical facts, data, examples, or sources you use to support your claim. Evidence must be relevant, specific, and accurate.

Types of evidence in history

  • Primary sources: Texts, artifacts, art, laws, treaties from the period
  • Statistical data: Population figures, trade volumes, economic indicators
  • Specific events: Battles, treaties, reforms, inventions — with dates
  • Scholarly interpretation: Historian's analysis or historiographical debate

Evidence selection by PIECES theme

  • GOV: Treaties, constitutions, decrees, election records
  • TEC: Inventions, patents, infrastructure records, scientific texts
  • ENV: Geographic data, climate records, agricultural output, disease records
  • CDI: Religious texts, artwork, philosophy, literary works
  • ECN: Trade ledgers, tax records, GDP data, labor statistics
  • SIO: Census data, legal codes on social status, letters, diaries
Example

✗ Vague "Trade increased along the Silk Road."

✓ Specific "Marco Polo's account (c.1298) describes Mongol relay stations (yam) positioned every 25 miles, which reduced travel time between Beijing and Tabriz from over a year to approximately six months."

🔗 Reasoning Why it matters

Reasoning is the logical explanation that connects your evidence to your claim. This is where most students struggle — they present evidence but never explain why it proves their point.

Strong reasoning does three things

  • Explains: Why does this evidence support the claim?
  • Analyzes: What PIECES theme does it connect to?
  • Extends: What broader historical significance does it reveal?
Reasoning template

"This evidence demonstrates [PIECES theme] because [mechanism/explanation]. This is historically significant because [broader pattern or consequence], which ultimately [connection to claim]."

Reasoning sentence starters

"This demonstrates that…"
"This is significant because…"
"This reveals a broader pattern of…"
"As a consequence of this…"
"This connects to [PIECES theme] by…"
"The underlying cause was…"

How CER + PIECES Work Together

PIECES provides the analytical lenses. CER provides the argument structure. Together, they turn raw historical knowledge into rigorous, multi-dimensional analysis.

PIECES lens → Claim → Evidence → Reasoning

Start by choosing a PIECES theme as your analytical lens. Use it to frame a precise claim. Select evidence that matches the theme. Then write reasoning that explicitly connects the evidence to the theme and explains its broader significance.

Full CER paragraph (Silk Road + ECN/CDI)

Claim: The Mongol Empire's Pax Mongolica restructured Eurasian economic systems by securing overland trade.

Evidence: Marco Polo's account describes hundreds of relay stations (yam) every 25 miles, cutting travel from Beijing to Tabriz from a year to six months.

Reasoning: This infrastructure demonstrates how political stability (GOV) enabled economic transformation (ECN) — by guaranteeing merchant safety, the Mongols lowered the cost and risk of long-distance trade. The resulting increase in commercial activity facilitated cultural exchange (CDI), as merchants carried not only goods but religious ideas, scientific knowledge, and artistic traditions across civilizational boundaries.
